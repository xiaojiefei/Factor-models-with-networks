Abstract:
Does inter-industry risk contagion in China operate through a single channel? We
argue it does not. Traditional models often mix supply-chain transmission with financial
leverage amplification. Using a five-dimensional coupled network framework across 31
Chinese industries from 2007 to 2023, we document a dual-track mechanism
supported by the near-zero correlation between price and tail risks. Our results show
that the dominant contagion channel switches depending on the market regime: (1)
The entity-driven channel (measured by price risk) functions as a structural backbone.
It reflects continuous supply-chain transmission and maintains stable dominance
during both normal periods and real-economy shocks (e.g., trade friction), with
technology sectors acting as risk absorbers; (2) The financial-driven channel
(measured by tail risk) is crisis activated. Typically dormant, it erupts explosively during
leverage-fueled crises (e.g., the 2015 crash), characterized by bidirectional
amplification loops and the shift of non-bank financial institutions to core spillover roles.
Mechanistic analysis reveals that this financial fragility is rooted in a structural “finance-
property closed loop”, where economic financialization and real estate dependence
disproportionately amplify tail risk contagion. Consequently, effective regulation
demands a dual-track approach: routine surveillance for supply-chain risks and
preemptive measures for leverage-driven financial contagion.

1. Introduction 
Does inter-industry risk contagion in China flow through a single channel? We argue it does 
not. Consider two typical shocks: a surge in production costs for a steel manufacturer (a real-
economy shock) versus a leverage collapse in a securities firm (a financial-sector shock). The former 
transmits primarily through supply chains, whereas the latter spreads via financial leverage. 
However, traditional risk contagion models, often based on single-dimensional frameworks like 
volatility spillovers (Diebold and Yılmaz, 2014), tend to mix these distinct mechanisms. This 
overlooks a fundamental empirical reality: distinct risk dimensions—specifically price changes 
versus tail events—can be statistically orthogonal and operate through different network structures. 
Treating these dimensions as interchangeable fails to capture the heterogeneous nature of systemic 
risk, posing challenges for risk management in emerging markets where the divergence between 
real-economy and financial-sector shocks is particularly pronounced. 
As the world’s second-largest economy and a representative emerging capital market, China 
exhibits unique institutional features that require a tailored analytical framework. First, China’s 
ongoing transition from a bank-dominated system to a multi-tiered capital market has diversified

how different risk dimensions operate through distinct channels, offering insights with both regional 
and global implications given China’s systemic spillover potential in Asia’s financial connectivity 
(Glick and Hutchison, 2013; Morelli and Vioto, 2020). 
Our central thesis is that inter-industry risk contagion in China operates through two 
fundamentally distinct tracks. The first is a structural, entity-driven channel, measured by price risk, 
which continuously transmits shocks via supply chains. The second is a financial-driven channel, 
measured by tail risk. This channel typically remains dormant during normal periods but becomes 
the dominant force during systemic crises. We argue that these two channels differ significantly in 
their timing, network structure, and economic sensitivity. Specifically, the entity-driven channel 
reflects the steady transmission of costs and demand along production networks. In contrast, the 
financial-driven channel is triggered by extreme events, such as the 2015 stock market crash, driven 
by leverage unwinding, liquidity freezes, and market panic. 
To empirically disentangle these dual racks, we address three limitations in existing literature. 
First, prior studies mainly focus on risk contagion within financial sectors (Nguyen et al., 2020) or 
across national stock markets (Abuzayed et al., 2021). This leaves a gap in understanding the 
spillover mechanisms between the financial sector and the real economy. Given the close link 
between financial market volatility and real economic performance, coupled with risk transmission 
through trade networks (Acemoglu et al., 2012), the analysis must extend beyond financial 
subsectors. To address this, we constructed a comprehensive dataset covering 31 sectors in China, 
spanning both financial industries (such as banking and non-bank finance) and real-economy sectors 
(such as manufacturing and technology), to systematically investigate these cross-sector risk 
transmission chains. 
Second, few studies combine dynamic and static perspectives to characterize how risk 
contagion evolves over time, especially during market transitions. Capturing this temporal variation 
requires a framework that can identify both persistent structural patterns and sudden, crisis-specific 
dynamics. Therefore, we implement a dual strategy: (1) a dynamic analysis using rolling-window 
networks to track the continuous evolution of risk contagion from 2007 to 2023, revealing how the 
dominance of risk dimensions shifts; and (2) a static analysis focusing on five key historical 
periods—such as the global financial crisis and the China-US trade friction—to isolate specific 
contagion patterns through case studies. 
Third, existing network approaches often rely on linear frameworks and traditional Granger 
causality tests. These methods struggle to capture the nonlinear characteristics, structural breaks, 
and asymmetric dependence patterns inherent in financial systems (Shahzad et al., 2018; Chen and 
Sun, 2024). Moreover, traditional unidimensional frameworks based on volatility or correlation 
metrics (Diebold and Yılmaz, 2014) fail to capture how transmission mechanisms diverge across 
different risk dimensions during extreme events.

To overcome these limitations, we develop a five-dimensional risk framework integrating price 
risk, volatility risk, asymmetric risk, extreme risk, and tail risk. Our approach involves three key 
steps: (1) using multivariate nonlinear Granger causality tests with a leave-one-out (LOO) approach  
to construct distinct subnetworks for each risk dimension (Hué et al., 2019); (2) applying network 
coupling methods to estimate time-varying weights that quantify the dominance of each dimension 
in driving systemic risk (Bonaccolto et al., 2019; Billio et al., 2023); and (3) calculating edge 
uniqueness to identify contagion paths specific to a single dimension (Musmeci et al., 2017). This 
framework enables us to test our central hypothesis: if the two channels are distinct, the price risk 
network (entity channel) should show persistently high uniqueness, while the tail risk network 
(financial channel) should exhibit explosive surges in uniqueness and influence only during crises. 
Our empirical findings support the dual-track hypothesis through three layers of evidence. First, 
dynamic network analysis establishes the temporal separation between the two channels. By 
decomposing the network, we find a fundamental distinction: Price risk networks (the entity channel) 
exhibit persistently high uniqueness and stable coupling weights across all periods. This confirms 
that supply-chain transmission operates continuously as a structural backbone. In contrast, tail risk 
networks (the financial channel) are crisis-specific. Their uniqueness and coupling weights are low 
during tranquil periods but surge explosively during the 2008 financial crisis and the 2015 stock 
market crash. Sectoral data supports this: cyclical industries maintain a stable risk contribution (25-
35%), while the financial sector’s contribution spikes (>40%) only during crises. Notably, during 
the COVID-19 pandemic, while tail risk uniqueness rose, its dominance (weight) remained 
moderate, confirming the pandemic as primarily a supply-chain shock rather than a leverage crisis. 
Second, case studies validate that shock origin determines which channel activates. We analyze 
two contrasting periods. The China-US trade friction (2018-2020) represents a typical entity-driven 
shock. Risk propagated one-way through supply chains, where technology and export-oriented 
sectors acted as a "risk sink," accumulating stress transmitted from upstream industries due to direct 
trade restrictions, while tail risk remained subdued. Network topology during this period reflects 
precise, industry-specific transmission along production linkages—distinctly different from 
systemic financial contagion patterns. Conversely, the 2015 stock market crash represents a 
financial-driven shock fueled by excessive leverage. Here, tail risk became overwhelmingly 
dominant. Non-bank financial institutions shifted from peripheral roles to become core risk 
spreaders, and the roles of the real sectors are fragmented. This comparative analysis demonstrates 
that transmission channels are not static network properties but dynamically activated depending on 
whether shocks originate from real-economy disruptions or financial-system instabilities. 
Third, mechanistic analysis identifies why the financial channel is so fragile. Having 
established that entity-driven and financial-driven channels exhibit distinct patterns, we investigate 
what macroeconomic factors render the financial channel explosively fragile during crises. 
Controlling for network effects, we find that economic financialization and real estate prosperity 
significantly amplify contagion intensity. Our analysis suggests these factors specifically fuel the 
financial-driven channel rather than the entity-driven one. This is evidenced by the asymmetry 
between periods: during the 2015 crash, high financialization levels coincided with the explosive 
activation of tail risk and the dominance of non-bank financial institutions. Conversely, during the 
trade friction period, these macro-financial vulnerabilities remained dormant, and risk followed

supply chains. This vulnerability stems from a structural "finance-property closed loop". High 
financialization concentrates leverage in shadow banking, while real estate credit links property 
cycles to systemic stability. Once triggered by panic or deleveraging, this loop acts as an amplifier, 
igniting the explosive contagion observed in our tail risk networks. In contrast, the entity-driven 
channel operates through physical production linkages. It lacks this leverage-based amplification 
mechanism, which explains why it maintains structural stability even when financial risks erupt. 
These findings have profound implications for financial regulation and systemic risk 
prevention in emerging markets. Our evidence suggests that effective risk management demands a 
dual-track regulatory approach tailored to the distinct mechanisms of each channel: (1) For the 
entity-driven channel, policymakers should establish supply-chain-based early-warning systems,  
leveraging industry price indices to monitor structural and persistent risk transmission—a task of 
routine surveillance; (2) For the financial-driven channel, regulators must prioritize macroprudential 
policies targeting financial leverage and real estate financing to preempt the activation of tail risk 
contagion—a critical focus for crisis prevention. Ultimately, single-track regulatory frameworks 
that treat all risk dimensions equivalently will prove inadequate in addressing the heterogeneous 
nature of systemic risk in complex, interconnected financial systems.

risk contagion, focusing on risk carrier, contagion dimensions, and identification methods. Section 
3 details the dataset and the multidimensional network methodology. Section 4 presents the 
empirical results, including dynamic network evolution, static case studies, and mechanistic analysis. 
Finally, Section 5 concludes the paper with policy implications. 
2. Literature review 
This paper is closely associated with the study of financial risk, particularly the application of 
complex networks to the analysis of financial risk contagion. By categorizing risk carriers, 
contagion dimensions, and identification methodologies, the existing literature has conducted a 
systematic investigation into financial risk contagion. 
From the perspective of risk carriers, most literature focuses on the contagion of financial risks 
in transnational stock markets. Risk indicators are constructed by using the return data of broad-
based stock indexes of various countries in different periods, and risk transmission models are 
designed to analyze the transnational contagion mechanism of systemic risks, and the relationship 
between financial risk contagion channels and economic states is studied. For example, the risk 
information transmission mode under special periods such as the international financial crisis and 
the COVID-19 pandemic (Abuzayed et al., 2021). Some studies note the complexity of the 
composition of global financial markets and believe that the cross-market and cross-asset 
transmission path should also be included as an important factor affecting the stability of a country’s 
financial market (Huang and Liu, 2023). However, financial risks will not only be transmitted across 
regions and markets, but there is a more refined risk aggregation and contagion mechanism at the 
medium and micro levels of the industry (Yao and Jiang, 2025). Risk events in a single industry 
have strong externalities, and the chain reaction of risk contagion between industries will lead to the 
outbreak of systemic risks (Acemoglu et al., 2012). Especially in the financial market, investors’

irrational imitation behavior may trigger market panic, expand the impact intensity of heterogeneous 
shocks, and form cross-sector and cross-industry financial risk contagion (Avery and Zemsky, 1998). 
However, a large number of existing studies mostly focus on risk contagion among sub-
industries of the financial sector, such as the exploration of risk contagion among different banks, 
securities brokers, and insurance companies (Yang et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2025). These studies 
believe that the risks in the financial industry were the root cause of global risks, but did not include 
the real economy industry or the financial industry in the research scope. Different sectors can exert 
influence through balance sheet channels, especially between the real and financial sectors. 
Specifically, when a single entity or industry is faced with a crisis, enterprises will sell assets in a 
concentrated manner to ensure liquidity and cause a sharp depreciation of related assets, thus leading 
to a contraction of balance sheets within the industry (Carvalho, 2015). The decline in the value of 
mortgage assets will further aggravate the vulnerability of the financial sector; thus, the transmission 
path of risks in the real economy to the financial sector is formed (Giannetti and Saidi, 2019). 
Especially in recent years, due to the intensified changes in the international situation and the 
frequent occurrence of public emergencies, the supply relationship of the industrial chain between 
industries is extremely vulnerable to impact, which leads to the rapid spread of extreme risks in 
individual industries to the financial market and the real economy in a short period. This necessitates 
adopting cross-industry risk contagion as the focal point, integrating real-economy sectors and 
financial industries into a unified framework to more comprehensively and precisely characterize 
risk transmission mechanisms in markets. 
From the perspective of contagion dimensions, traditional financial risk measurement 
primarily focuses on the mean and volatility of asset returns, namely the first and second moments. 
By investigating correlations between the first and second moments across assets, researchers 
analyze contagion pathways of financial risks. For instance, Asgharian and Nossman (2011) 
employed a stochastic volatility model to study risk spillover mechanisms from the U.S. stock 
market to European stock markets through the second-moment dimension. Yang and Zhou (2017) 
examined the impact of quantitative easing on global stock market volatility spillover patterns from 
the second-moment perspective. Gao et al. (2021) investigated time-varying first- and second-
moment connectedness networks between Chinese green bonds and equities, commodities, foreign 
exchange, and money markets under financial event-driven scenarios. 
With the continuous development of China’s financial markets and the gradual introduction of 
short-selling mechanisms and derivatives, scholars have begun to focus on the dimension of tail risk 
(Ando et al., 2022). Compared to mean and volatility levels, tail risk exhibits stronger contagion 
effects and broader transmission scopes. Once major adverse events occur, tail risk propagates 
through economic and financial industry linkage networks, threatening the stability of the entire 
financial-economic system. In recent years, frequent extreme risk events and persistent volatility in 
international financial markets have revealed limitations in existing risk dimensions for 
comprehensively characterizing complex intermarket return distribution linkages and asymmetries. 
For instance, regarding the second moment, variance simplistically equates losses and gains, failing 
to fully represent real and refined risk measurement, as downside risks typically exhibit higher 
realization probabilities than upside risks (Bouri and Jalkh, 2023). In light of this, emerging research 
suggests that a substantial portion of risk information transmission can be reflected through higher-

order moment spillovers. There is growing consensus on incorporating asymmetric risk and extreme 
risk dimensions, utilizing kurtosis and skewness characteristics of financial asset return distributions 
to capture extreme event frequencies and pricing asymmetries (Neuberger and Payne, 2021). 
Theoretically, kurtosis spillovers reflect the cross-market propagation of extreme event risks, while 
skewness spillovers measure the transmission of return distribution asymmetries across markets 
(Bouri et al., 2021; Bouri and Jalkh, 2023). 
While existing studies have increasingly adopted complex network methodologies, real-world 
risk contagion across financial markets or real economic sectors often manifests more intricate 
patterns. These processes typically exhibit multi-layer network structures characterized by multi-
dimensional, hybridized interconnections and time-varying complex linkage effects (Bargigli et al., 
2015). Multiple forms of risk contagion may erupt simultaneously, with sophisticated interaction 
mechanisms operating across different network layers. Consequently, risk contagion networks 
constructed solely on single dimensions fail to reflect authentic transmission mechanisms or 
accurately capture real-world risk diffusion patterns. Instead, researchers should employ scientific 
approaches like coupled networks to develop multi-layer synthetic frameworks that capture cross-
layer interaction dynamics and identify complex financial risk transmission pathways (Wang et al., 
2021). 
From the perspective of identification methodologies, on one hand, to verify the existence of 
intermarket risk contagion relationships, (Hong et al., 2009) pioneered the application of Granger 
causality tests in financial risk measurement, examining linear risk spillovers between pairwise 
financial assets. However, early-stage studies predominantly focused on pairwise interaction 
mechanisms between financial entities, neglecting the holistic network connectivity of financial 
risks, thereby failing to accurately characterize systemic shocks emerging within financial networks 
(van de Leur et al., 2017). When research scope expands to multiple financial assets or real-sector 
industries, this necessitates capturing both the heterogeneity and interconnectedness of risk 
transmission processes. Consequently, complex network models have garnered increasing scholarly 
attention, offering robust analytical tools for delineating risk correlations among financial entities 
and assessing financial system fragility (Denbee et al., 2021). These network-based approaches 
enable the identification of systemically important nodes, measurement of risk propagation channels, 
and detection of contagion path dependencies through topological analysis of financial network 
architectures. 
However, existing literature employing network correlation approaches to investigate financial 
risks predominantly operates within linear frameworks combined with traditional Granger causality 
testing methods. In financial systems, asset return series often exhibit significant nonlinear 
characteristics and structural breaks due to factors such as fire sales of commonly held assets (Kyle 
and Xiong, 2001; Duarte and Eisenbach, 2021), panic-driven systemic risk contagion among 
investors (Barunik et al., 2020), and asymmetric dependence between asset returns during market 
upswings versus downturns (Bollerslev et al., 2022). Furthermore, risk spillover effects demonstrate 
pronounced asymmetry in their responses to changing market conditions, with financial risk 
contagion frequently manifesting nonlinear propagation patterns (Shahzad et al., 2018; Chen and 
Sun, 2024). To address these complexities, researchers have incorporated multivariate nonlinear 
Granger causality methods into risk contagion network construction, accounting for the tail

clustering effects and asymmetric nature of financial risks (Diks and Wolski, 2016). Additionally, 
when analyzing networks containing numerous nodes, scholars have introduced the leave-one-out 
approach to enhance causal relationship identification amidst substantial third-party interference 
(Zedda and Cannas, 2020). This methodology systematically calculates causal relationships while 
controlling for each third-party market, thereby improving network structure characterization. 
On the other hand, the majority of existing studies utilize vector autoregressive (VAR) models 
to investigate influencing factors of market risk contagion after constructing risk transmission 
networks (Huang and Liu, 2023). Some research enhances the basic VAR framework with advanced 
estimation techniques, including error correction models, frequency domain decomposition 
methods, and time-varying coefficient approaches (Favero, 2013; Antonakakis et al., 2018; Baruník 
and Křehlík, 2018). However, traditional VAR models face inherent limitations in handling 
excessive node quantities and time-varying contagion structures within networks, often leading to 
unreliable estimation outcomes when applied to complex financial systems. To address these 
challenges, (Zhu et al., 2017) pioneered the network autoregressive (NAR) model, which effectively 
resolves the “curse of dimensionality” plaguing conventional VAR frameworks. Unlike traditional 
models where parameter counts grow exponentially with node numbers, the NAR specification 
maintains stable parameter dimensionality regardless of network size. This breakthrough enables 
robust analysis of ultra-high-dimensional financial time series data across multiple industries. 
3. Data and Methodology 
3.1. Data and sample description 
We employ the indices based on the Shenwan Level-1 industry classification. The Shenwan 
industry classification system is built upon four core principles: “profit-driven, valuation clustering, 
physical attributes, and conventional industry practices” to establish its classification framework. 
The characteristics of these indices are as follows: they align with investors’ intuitive understanding, 
ensure that companies within the same industry share comparable business models, financial metrics, 
and market performance, and undergo timely adjustments in response to China’s economic 
development and the continuous evolution of the securities market, thereby ensuring well-grounded 
practicality of the industry definitions. The Shenwan Level-1 industry indices comprise 31 
industries (Xu et al., 2022), the full names and abbreviations of which are provided in Appendix A 
(Table A1). 
We adopt the period from January 2007 to December 2023 as the sample period. To analyze 
risk contagion patterns during distinct market regimes, we partition the sample into five periods 
based on major economic and financial events. Period A—August 2007 to December 2009—
encompasses the global financial crisis, during which global credit markets experienced severe 
disruptions and contagion effects propagated across international markets (Brunnermeier, 2009; 
Longstaff, 2010). Period B—July 2014 to June 2015, and Period C—June 2015 to January 2016—
capture China’s dramatic bull-to-bear market transition, characterized by leverage-fueled 
speculative bubbles (Bian et al., 2018; Allen et al., 2019). Period D—March 2018 to January 2020—
covers the China-US trade friction episode, during which escalating tariffs disrupted supply chains 
and created uncertainty (Fajgelbaum et al., 2020; Fetzer and Schwarz, 2021). Period E—December

3.2. Research methodology 
3.2.1. Multidimensional risk measurement method

through distinct mechanisms—we must first quantify five risk dimensions that potentially capture 
different aspects of contagion: price risk (reflecting direct cost transmission), volatility risk 
(uncertainty propagation), asymmetric risk (downside contagion asymmetries), extreme risk (rare 
event spillovers), and tail risk (systemic leverage-driven contagion). Traditional single-dimensional 
approaches based solely on volatility or correlation cannot differentiate whether observed contagion 
reflects supply-chain mechanisms, financial panic, or both, thereby motivating our 
multidimensional measurement framework.

For tail risk measurement, we employ Conditional Value at Risk (CoVaR) following Pham et 
al. (2024). CoVaR quantifies systemic interdependence by measuring the maximum potential loss 
in one industry conditional on another industry experiencing extreme stress—a feature for 
identifying financial-driven contagion where leverage amplification creates nonlinear tail 
dependencies distinct from linear supply-chain transmission. We integrate CoVaR within our 
multidimensional framework rather than using it as a standalone measure, because isolated CoVaR 
analysis cannot distinguish whether tail dependencies stem from entity-driven linkages or financial-
driven panic (Fengler and Gisler, 2015). It is essential to distinguish between fourth-moment-based 
extreme risk measurements (Bouri and Jalkh, 2023), which capture entity-specific rare event 
characteristics, and CoVaR-derived tail dependence risk (Pham et al., 2024), which quantifies 
systemic interdependence between individual industries and the overall market. The inherent 
conceptual divergence between these risk dimensions, as empirically demonstrated by the distinct 
temporal patterns revealed in our subsequent analyses, necessitates their coupled examination 
through an integrated framework. The detailed CoVaR estimation procedure using DCC-GARCH 
models (Engle, 2002) is provided in Appendix B.

fundamentally distinct risk characteristics. This near-orthogonality supports our dual-track 
hypothesis: if price risk and tail risk were highly correlated, entity-driven and financial-driven

(2) Block model analysis 
The block model is a framework for describing the community structure of networks, which 
divides nodes into groups based on their attributes or behavioral rules, forming several 
“communities” or “blocks” within a complex network. This method not only identifies the nodes 
contained in each block but also analyzes the role each block plays in a risk contagion network 
(Zhang et al., 2020). The block model classifies network blocks into four distinct roles: First, the 
Main Benefit, where members receive both internal and external links but obtain more links within 
the block than from outside, maintaining a small proportion of external relations. Second, the Main 
Spillover, characterized by members that send many links to other blocks while exchanging few 
links internally and receiving minimal external connections. Third, the Bilateral Spillover, whose 
members actively send numerous links both inside and outside their blocks while receiving few 
external links in return. Finally, the Broker, where members both send and receive external links 
while maintaining few internal connections. 
Our block model analysis account for how a block’s relative size influences its internal 
relationship count, where larger blocks inherently contain more internal connections, whereas 
smaller blocks demonstrate the inverse pattern (Wasserman and Faust, 1994). To accurately 
evaluate a block’s relationship preferences, one should compare the actual proportion of internal 
relationships against the expected proportion, while accounting for both the possible relationships 
within the block and across the entire network.

4. Results and analysis 
4.1. Dynamic analysis of multidimensional risk contagion networks 
Using time-series data of five-dimensional risks across industries, we construct dynamic stock 
market risk networks through nonlinear multivariate Granger causality tests based on the leave-one-
out approach. Following established practices in the Chinese market literature, we employ a rolling 
window of 250 trading days (approximately one trading year) with a sliding step of 5 trading days, 
yielding weekly frequency networks from 2007 to 2023. This framework generates five dimension-
specific causal networks—price risk, volatility risk, asymmetric risk, extreme risk, and tail risk 
networks—alongside a synthesized multidimensional coupled network that integrates information 
across all dimensions. The analysis proceeds in three stages: first, we examine when systemic stress 
emerges through aggregate network topology; second, we disentangle why stress emerges by 
isolating dimension-specific transmission mechanisms via edge uniqueness analysis; third, we 
validate these mechanisms through sectoral contribution patterns. 
4.1.1. Coupled network topology evolution 
Fig. 1 illustrates the temporal evolution of these three metrics. Several patterns emerge from 
the data. First, all three metrics exhibit synchronized fluctuations, suggesting that changes in global

connectivity, local clustering, and node centrality reflect common underlying forces rather than 
independent structural shifts. Second, during exogenous shock periods—notably the global financial 
crisis and the COVID-19 pandemic—the network demonstrates what we term a “triple-high” 
configuration. Network density rises substantially, indicating proliferation of cross-sector linkages; 
clustering coefficients increase, signaling strengthened community structures; and eigenvector 
centrality becomes more concentrated, reflecting the dominance of systemically important nodes. 
These patterns are consistent with theoretical predictions that external shocks propagate risks 
through multiple channels—capital flow disruptions, supply chain fractures, and sentiment 
contagion—amplified by leverage effects and policy intervention lags (Chan, 2017; Yu et al., 2024). 
Third, during endogenous bull-to-bear transitions (Period B: July 2014 to June 2015; Period C: 
June 2015 to January 2016), the metrics display sharp “V-shaped” reversals. All three indicators 
decline rapidly during the bull market phase, then surge explosively following the market crash. 
This pattern suggests that leverage-driven risks remained concealed under market euphoria, only to 
erupt when sentiment reversed or deleveraging policies triggered liquidity crunches. Notably, while 
risk metrics moderated during the post-2015 regulatory reforms and the China-US trade friction 
period (Period D: March 2018 to January 2020), they did not return to pre-crisis baselines, indicating 
persistent structural vulnerabilities. 
These topological patterns document when the risk network intensifies but cannot explain the 
underlying mechanisms. The coupled network aggregates all five risk dimensions, obscuring 
whether intensification reflects supply-chain disruptions propagating price shocks, financial panic 
amplifying tail dependencies, or both operating simultaneously. This limitation motivates our 
decomposition analysis in the following subsection.

4.1.2. Dimension-specific transmission mechanisms 
Having established aggregate temporal patterns, we now disentangle heterogeneous 
transmission mechanisms by examining edge uniqueness across the five risk dimensions. Edge 
uniqueness quantifies the extent to which risk transmission pathways in one dimension are distinct 
from those in other dimensions (Musmeci et al., 2017). Higher values indicate that a specific risk 
dimension possesses contagion channels unexplained by other metrics, thereby offering incremental 
information about systemic risk propagation. This analysis constitutes the core test of our dual-track 
hypothesis: if entity-driven and financial-driven channels operate through fundamentally distinct 
mechanisms, we should observe systematically different edge uniqueness patterns for price risk (our 
proxy for supply-chain transmission) versus tail risk (our proxy for financial-channel contagion). 
Fig. 2 presents the dynamic evolution of edge uniqueness for all five risk dimensions from 
2007 to 2023. The results reveal striking heterogeneity. Price risk networks consistently maintain 
elevated edge uniqueness throughout the sample period, with values generally exceeding those of 
other dimensions except during extreme market turmoil. This persistence suggests that cost and 
demand transmission along supply chains generates contagion pathways that remain structurally 
distinct from those captured by volatility, skewness, or kurtosis metrics, regardless of market regime. 
The stability of price risk uniqueness reflects the continuous nature of real-economy production 
networks: industries remain interconnected through input-output linkages that propagate price 
shocks via cost transmission mechanisms, even when financial markets transition between boom 
and crash cycles. 
In sharp contrast, tail risk networks exhibit dramatically different temporal dynamics. During 
tranquil periods—including most of the pre-crisis years, the bull market phase, and the China-US 
trade friction period—tail risk uniqueness remains subdued, often ranking among the lowest across 
all five dimensions. This dormancy indicates that tail dependencies largely overlap with patterns 
captured by other risk metrics during normal market conditions. However, during another three 
episodes—the bear market phase, segments of the COVID-19 pandemic, and portions of the global 
financial crisis—tail risk uniqueness surges to levels comparable to or exceeding price risk 
uniqueness. These eruptions signal activation of a crisis-specific transmission mechanism: systemic 
tail dependencies driven by leverage unwinding, liquidity freezes, and panic-induced correlation 
breakdowns that create contagion pathways qualitatively different from gradual supply-chain 
adjustments. 
The remaining three dimensions—volatility risk, asymmetric risk, and extreme risk—generally 
exhibit moderate edge uniqueness with limited temporal variation. Their contagion pathways largely 
overlap with those of price and tail risk, suggesting these dimensions capture intermediate or 
composite aspects of transmission rather than constituting independent channels. Notably, extreme 
risk (kurtosis-based) displays modest increases during crises but remains substantially below tail

entity-driven transmission through supply chains operates continuously as a structural feature of 
inter-industry risk propagation. Conversely, the crisis-specific eruptions of tail risk uniqueness 
demonstrate that financial-driven transmission activates only under extreme stress, becoming the 
dominant channel when leverage amplification and panic contagion override gradual cost

amplification activates. Such dominance represents a phenomenon fundamentally distinct from the

4.1.3 Sectoral contribution patterns 
To validate the dual-track mechanism identified through edge uniqueness analysis, we examine 
whether sectoral risk contributions align with the predicted patterns. If price risk reflects continuous 
supply-chain transmission, sectors deeply embedded in production networks should contribute 
persistently regardless of market regime. Conversely, if tail risk activates during crises, the financial 
sector’s contribution should surge precisely when tail risk uniqueness peaks. 
We compute dynamically weighted average systemic importance measures for five major 
sector groups: Financial (banking and non-bank finance), Technology (electronics, computers, 
communications, media), Cyclical (oil & petrochemical, coal, steel, nonferrous metals, building 
materials, machinery, real estate, transportation), Consumption (food & beverage, textiles, 
household appliances, pharmaceuticals, agriculture), and Comprehensive (defense, utilities, public 
services). The 31 industries are categorized based on their positions in industrial chains. For each 
sector group, we calculate the weighted average of constituent industries’ systemic importance 
measures, using time-varying coupling weights to aggregate across risk dimensions. The relative 
contribution of each sector at time is computed as its weighted systemic importance divided by the 
sum across all five sectors. 
Fig. 4 presents the temporal evolution of sectoral risk contributions from 2007 to 2023. The 
results support the dual-track interpretation. First, the Cyclical sector maintains the highest and most 
stable contribution throughout nearly the entire sample period, generally ranging between 25% and 
35%. This stability aligns with our interpretation of price risk as reflecting continuous supply-chain 
transmission: cyclical industries form the backbone of China’s production networks, transmitting 
cost and demand shocks persistently through upstream-downstream linkages. The sector’s 
contribution exhibits only moderate increases during the global financial crisis and COVID-19 
pandemic, consistent with the relative stability of price risk uniqueness during these periods. Small 
fluctuations occur but do not fundamentally alter the sector’s dominant position, confirming that 
entity-driven transmission constitutes a structural, always-present channel. 
Second, the Financial sector’s contribution displays explosive crisis-specific surges that 
coincide precisely with tail risk uniqueness peaks. During the global financial crisis, the Financial 
sector’s contribution jumps substantially, driven by liquidity crunches in banking and leverage 
collapses in non-bank finance. Even more dramatically, during the bear market phase following the 
2015 bull market peak, the Financial sector’s contribution surges to levels exceeding 40%, 
dominating the risk transmission network as off-market margin financing unwound and stock pledge 
financing triggered forced liquidations. These eruptions occur precisely when tail risk uniqueness 
surges in Fig. 2, providing sectoral validation that financial-driven contagion becomes 
overwhelmingly dominant when systemic tail dependencies activate. Notably, during the China-US 
trade friction period—an entity-driven shock—the Financial sector’s contribution remains subdued 



=== 第22页 ===
 
20 
despite elevated overall risk, confirming that the financial channel remains dormant when crises do 
not involve leverage amplification or panic contagion. 
Third, the Technology sector exhibits an intriguing dual pattern: elevated contributions during 
both the 2015 crash and the trade friction period, but through different mechanisms suggested by 
the timing and co-movement with other sectors. During the crash, the Technology sector’s surge 
coincides with the Financial sector’s peak and tail risk activation, suggesting vulnerability through 
high valuations and speculative leverage—a financial-channel effect. During trade friction, the 
Technology sector’s elevated contribution occurs while tail risk uniqueness remains low and 
coincides with stable Cyclical sector contributions, indicating impact through export restrictions 
and supply chain restructuring—an entity-channel effect. This dual exposure demonstrates that 
some sectors participate in both transmission channels, though the mechanisms differ fundamentally. 
The Consumption sector maintains relatively stable contributions for most periods but exhibits 
increases during the COVID-19 pandemic, reflecting supply chain disruptions for essential goods. 
The Comprehensive sector displays delayed responses, with contributions rising after other sectors 
have peaked, potentially reflecting its role in absorbing residual risks through diversified industrial 
exposures. 
Taken together, the sectoral evidence corroborates the dual-track mechanism identified 
through edge uniqueness analysis. The Cyclical sector’s persistent dominance parallels price risk’s 
continuous elevation, while the Financial sector’s crisis-specific surges align with tail risk 
activations. This correspondence between dimension-specific edge uniqueness patterns and sectoral 
contribution dynamics provides convergent validation that entity-driven and financial-driven 
channels operate through fundamentally distinct mechanisms with different temporal characteristics.

4.2. Static analysis of multidimensional risk contagion networks 
The dynamic analysis provides temporal evidence that the multidimensional risk network 
exhibits patterns consistent with a dual-track contagion mechanism. To substantiate these dynamic 
findings and elucidate the underlying transmission mechanisms at the sectoral level, we now 
examine static network configurations during five historical periods. Rather than exhaustively 
documenting all periods, we organize our analysis around two contrasting case studies that represent 
fundamentally different shock origins. Throughout the following analysis, we employ standard 
abbreviations for industry names (e.g., ET for Electronics, NBF for Non-Bank Finance, CP for 
Computer) to enhance readability. The complete list of full names and abbreviations is provided in 
Appendix A (Table A1). 
4.2.1. Case 1: China-US trade friction—An entity-driven channel shock 
The China-US trade friction period (March 2018–January 2020) constitutes a typical entity-
driven channel shock. Trade policy disruptions directly impacted supply chains, production costs, 
and export-oriented sectors—mechanisms characteristic of entity-driven contagion. 
Following Zhang et al. (2020), we conduct block model analysis on the constructed five-
dimensional risk contagion networks, with inter-block interaction patterns and block classifications 
presented in Table 3. During the China-US trade friction, M1 and M2 were both classified as main 
benefit blocks. M1 included all technology sector industries (electronics, computer, 
communications, media), cyclical sector industries such as transportation and real estate, and 
consumption sector industries like pharmaceuticals and biotechnology, and food and beverage. The 
underlying mechanism lies in the direct shocks inflicted upon the technology sector during China-
US trade friction through technology embargoes, export restrictions, and supply chain restructuring, 
which significantly intensified its risk absorption capacity and positioned it as a risk absorption 
conduit (Zhou et al., 2025). For instance, core technological domains such as semiconductors and 
communication equipment were subjected to external policy suppression during this period. This 
not only concentrated risks internally within these industries but also heightened their vulnerability, 
leading to the absorption of additional spillover risks from other sectors. 
M2 primarily comprised some consumption and cyclical industries alongside the non-bank 
finance industry. These industries absorbed risks from other blocks during the trade friction phase 
due to external shocks. For instance, the nonferrous metals and chemical industries faced impacts 
from trade policy uncertainty and global supply chain volatility, while the non-bank finance industry 
became a primary absorber of external risks due to funding chain fluctuations. M3, classified as the 
main spillover block, included cyclical sectors such as machinery equipment and automobile 
industries, the banking industry, and the comprehensive industry. The machinery and automobile 
industries exhibited significant risk spillovers under tariff pressures and export dependencies, while 
the banking industry intensified risk transmission capabilities amid exchange rate volatility and 
liquidity tightening. M4, designated as the bilateral spillover block, encompassed oil and 
petrochemical, architectural ornament, electrical equipment, national defense, and steel industries. 
The oil and petrochemical and steel industries functioned as both risk sources and transmitters under 
dual pressures of global trade dynamics and commodity price fluctuations. Concurrently, the 



=== 第24页 ===
 
22 
national defense industry demonstrated strong bidirectional risk interactions, reflecting heightened 
geopolitical tensions between China and the U.S. during this period. 
The image matrix for Period D (Fig. 5) reveals a unidirectional transmission pattern 
characteristic of supply chain propagation. Block 4 (bilateral spillover, containing upstream 
manufacturing) transmits risks predominantly toward Block 1 (main benefit, containing technology 
sectors). Block 2 (main benefit) receives inputs from Block 4 but exhibits minimal outward 
transmission. This unidirectional flow from upstream manufacturing to downstream absorption 
sectors validates the supply chain logic underlying entity-driven contagion. 
Building on the block-level analysis, we examine individual industry roles within the 
multidimensional risk contagion networks (see Fig. 6). Industries are classified into four roles based 
on their in-degree and out-degree rankings: Risk Drivers (low in-degree, high out-degree), Risk 
Recipients (high in-degree, low out-degree), Risk Propagators (high in-degree, high out-degree), 
and Risk Isolates (low in-degree, low out-degree). Fig. 6 Panel D maps these roles across the China-
US trade friction period. The four technology industries—ET (Electronics), CP (Computer), CMC 
(Communications), and MD (Media)—are all concentrated in the right half of the diagram, 
indicating high in-degree rankings (absorbing substantial risks from other sectors). Specifically, ET, 
CP, CMC, and MD occupy positions with out-degree rankings between 0-5 and in-degree rankings 
between 5-15, placing them predominantly in the Risk Recipients quadrant. This cohesive clustering 
confirms that technology industries collectively functioned as risk absorbers during the trade friction. 
In contrast, upstream manufacturing industries occupy distinctly different positions. TA 
(Textile and Apparel), AM (Automobile Manufacturing), and AFAHF (Agriculture, Forestry, 
Animal Husbandry and Fishery) locate in the upper-left quadrant (low in-degree, low-to-moderate 
out-degree), functioning as Risk Drivers. Meanwhile, the non-bank financial industry (NBF) and 
banking industry (BA) occupy moderate positions without exhibiting dominant transmission roles—
NBF has an in-degree ranking around 15 and out-degree ranking around 24, while BA sits at 
approximately in-degree 10 and out-degree 11. The systemic importance measures, indicated by 
node sizes in Fig. 6 Panel D, reveal that consumption and cyclical sectors (shown in red and orange) 
such as SS (Steel), FB (Food and Beverage), BC (Building and Construction), and HA (Household 
Appliances) exhibit relatively large node sizes, reflecting their centrality in production networks. 
Notably, the largest nodes are AFAHF, OP (Oil and Petrochemical), FB, and SL (Steel), all cyclical 
or consumption sectors deeply embedded in supply chains. In contrast, NBF and BA display 
moderate node sizes, indicating that leverage-based importance remained subdued when financial 
channels were dormant. 
These static patterns align with the finding that tail risk edge uniqueness remained subdued 
during Period D (Fig. 2). The concentration of technology industries in risk absorption roles, the 
unidirectional transmission from upstream manufacturing sectors, and the peripheral positioning of 
financial institutions collectively validate that the China-US trade friction operated primarily 
through the entity-driven channel, where risks propagated along supply chain linkages rather than 
through financial intermediation. 
4.2.2. Case 2: 2015 stock market crash—A financial-driven channel shock 
The 2015 stock market crash (June 2015–January 2016) represents a typical financial-driven 
channel shock. Triggered by aggressive deleveraging policies that unwound margin financing, this

23 
crisis emanated from leverage concentration rather than real economic fundamentals—mechanisms 
characteristic of financial-driven contagion. 
Table 3 Period C documents a marked structural transformation from the trade friction period. 
Crucially, the four technology industries exhibited fragmented positioning: Computer (CP) in M2, 
while Electronics (ET), Communications (CMC), and Media (MD) all in M3. This dispersion 
contrasts sharply with their cohesive clustering in M1 during Period D, suggesting that financial 
panic disrupted sectoral coherence. Most notably, the non-bank financial industry (NBF) 
transitioned from M2 (main benefit) during trade friction to M4 (main spillover) during the crash, 
signaling its emergence as the primary source of systemic risk transmission. When leverage 
unwinding drives contagion, risks propagate through financial relationships—cross-holdings, 
margin calls, liquidity spirals—that cut across industrial boundaries, severing the supply chain logic 
that unified technology sectors under entity-driven shocks. 
The image matrix for Period C (Fig. 5) reveals a fundamentally different transmission topology. 
Unlike Period D’s unidirectional flows from upstream manufacturing to downstream absorption, 
Period C exhibits bidirectional amplification dynamics. Block 2 (main benefit) displays a prominent 
self-loop, indicating intense internal risk circulation among its members. Block 4 (main spillover, 
containing NBF) transmits risks upward toward Block 1 (main benefit), while Block 3 (main 
spillover, containing technology sectors) sends spillovers laterally to Block 2. Critically, Block 3 
receives flows from Block 2, creating a bidirectional exchange absent in the supply chain-driven 
Period D topology. This mutual amplification between blocks validates that risks propagated 
through financial linkages rather than production networks. 
Fig. 6 Panel C demonstrates how individual industries repositioned during the crash. The 
technology sector’s fragmentation becomes visually apparent: Electronics (ET) locates at in-degree 
24 and out-degree 5, Computer (CP) at in-degree 3 and out-degree 11, Communications (CMC) at 
in-degree 26 and out-degree 15, and Media (MD) at in-degree 25 and out-degree 16. This scattered 
distribution across different quadrants—with CP shifting rightward and upward while ET, CMC, 
MD remain dispersed in the left-center regions—contrasts starkly with their tight clustering in 
Period D’s right half, confirming that leverage exposure rather than supply chain position 
determined individual industries’ vulnerability during financial crises. 
These static patterns align precisely with the documentation of tail risk edge uniqueness 
surging dramatically during Period C (Fig. 2). The explosive activation of tail risk networks, 
combined with NBF’s transition from main benefit to main spillover, the bidirectional amplification 
flows in Fig. 5 Panel C (contrasting with unidirectional supply chain flows in Fig. 5 Panel D), and 
the fragmentation of previously cohesive technology sectors collectively validate that the 2015 crash 
operated predominantly through the financial-driven channel. Unlike trade friction’s supply chain 
propagation, the crash transmitted via leverage pyramids, liquidity spirals, and sentiment 
contagion—mechanisms captured by tail risk but not price risk networks. 
To sum up, the contrasts between Periods C and D substantiate the dual-track hypothesis 
through four dimensions. First, NBF’s role reversed from a main benefit block during trade friction 
to a main spillover block during the crash. This demonstrates that financial institutions’ function 
fundamentally shifts between entity-driven and financial-driven regimes. Second, technology 
sectors transformed from cohesive clustering in M1 during Period D to fragmentation across M2 



=== 第26页 ===
 
24 
and M3 during Period C. Their tight concentration in the right half of Fig. 6 Panel D contrasts with 
dispersion across quadrants in Fig. 6 Panel C, which reveals that supply chain coherence collapses 
under leverage-driven contagion. Third, the transmission topology inverted from unidirectional 
flows to bidirectional amplification loops. Fig. 5 Panel D shows Block 4 transmitting toward Block 
1, while Fig. 5 Panel C displays Block 2’s self-loop and mutual exchanges between Blocks 2 and 3. 
This confirms that entity-driven channels follow production networks, while financial-driven 
channels operate through self-reinforcing panic dynamics. Fourth, these static findings triangulate 
with the dynamic evidence, including the persistent high uniqueness of price risk networks versus 
explosive surges in tail risk networks, and the stable cyclical contributions versus crisis-period 
financial spikes. This convergence across multiple analytical approaches establishes that China’s 
inter-industry risk contagion operates through two coexisting yet distinct channels, each activated 
by different shock origins and transmitting through fundamentally different mechanisms.

level to address potential heteroskedasticity and intra-industry correlation in the regression 
coefficients. 
The dependent variables 𝑂𝑢𝑡𝑅𝑖𝑠𝑘, 𝐼𝑛𝑅𝑖𝑠𝑘, and 𝑅𝑖𝑠𝑘 represent the output intensity, input 
intensity, and overall intensity of multidimensional dynamic risk contagions in stock markets, 
respectively, which are hypothesized to be influenced by three primary factors. First, the cross-
industrial interaction-aggregation effect (𝛽) quantifies the aggregate impact of risk contagion 
intensities across all N industries on the contagion intensity of a single industry. If 𝛽= 0, the risk 
contagion intensity of individual industries operates independently or exhibits mutually offsetting 
effects, indicating no systemic risk interdependence. A negative 𝛽 ( 𝛽< 0 ) suggests a risk 
dispersion phenomenon, where contagion intensities across industries mutually attenuate, reducing 
cross-industrial vulnerability. Conversely, a positive 𝛽 (𝛽> 0) signifies risk aggregation, where 
contagion intensities synergistically amplify within the multidimensional risk network, leading to 
system-wide escalation. Second, the intra-industrial self-driven effect ( 𝛾) measures the 
autoregressive influence of the one-period lagged risk contagion intensity within industry i on its 
current intensity. A higher 𝛾 reflects stronger temporal persistence in risk propagation. 
Specifically, 𝛾> 0 indicates that risk accumulation within an industry exhibits inertia, persisting 
over time rather than dissipating rapidly, which implies that historical risk exposures propagate 
nonlinearly into future periods, sustaining prolonged systemic impacts.

variables across three dimensions: macroeconomic conditions, macro-financial environments, and

4.3.2. Macroeconomic drivers of the financial-driven channel 
Having established that entity-driven and financial-driven channels exhibit distinct activation 
patterns and transmission mechanisms, we now investigate what macroeconomic and institutional 
factors render the financial-driven channel particularly fragile and explosive during crises. The NAR 
model allows us to identify these structural vulnerabilities by controlling for network effects and 
isolating the impacts of China’s macroeconomic environment. 
Table 6 presents the NAR empirical results. Beyond the network interaction effects, two 
macroeconomic variables exhibit particularly robust positive impacts on risk contagion intensity: 
financialization degree (FinProp) and real estate prosperity (HouseIndex). The financialization 
degree consistently shows significant positive coefficients across all specifications—0.980, 0.738, 
and 0.880 (all significant at the 1% level) for risk output, input, and overall intensities respectively. 
This indicates that as China’s financial sector expands relative to GDP, systemic risk amplification 
intensifies. The mechanism operates through multiple channels. First, higher financialization 
concentrates leverage in financial institutions, creating potential for cascading failures when 
deleveraging occurs. Second, financialization diverts resources from productive sectors toward 
speculative activities, weakening the real economy’s shock-absorbing capacity. Third, complex 
financial instruments proliferate under high financialization, creating opaque interconnections that 
facilitate rapid contagion propagation once tail risks materialize. 
The positive influence of real estate prosperity (HouseIndex) on risk contagion fundamentally 
stems from the dual financial-real attributes of China’s property sector and the structural features of 
its economy. First, high-leverage-driven risk concentration: During periods of rising real estate 
prosperity, developers accelerate land acquisitions and project expansions, relying heavily on bank 
development loans, trust financing, and presale proceeds. Simultaneously, homebuyers increase 
leverage, leading to a rapid expansion of the financial system’s exposure to real estate risks. Second, 
resource diversion and economic structural fragility: High real estate prosperity attracts excessive 
capital inflows, crowding out financing opportunities for productive sectors such as manufacturing 
and technology. This reinforces financialization, diverting financial resources from productive 
investments to real estate speculation and eroding the endogenous growth momentum of the real 
economy. 
Importantly, financialization and real estate prosperity interact to form a “finance-real estate 
closed loop.” Risks become interlinked across markets through complex financial instruments such 
as Commercial Mortgage Backed Securities and supply chain asset-backed securities, resulting in 
concealed and hard-to-monitor contagion pathways. Notably, periods of high real estate prosperity 
are not immune to risks but rather stages of latent risk accumulation and accelerated cross-industry 
transmission. The synergy between real estate prosperity and financialization reflects the deepening 
of economic financialization—precisely the structural vulnerability that renders the financial-driven 
channel explosively fragile. 
These findings complete the evidentiary chain for our dual-track hypothesis. The dynamic and 
static analyses established that the financial-driven channel activates explosively during crises while 
remaining dormant during normal periods, transmitting risks through leverage-based linkages 
captured by tail risk networks. The NAR results reveal why this channel is so fragile: China’s 
macroeconomic structure—characterized by high financialization and a real estate-centric credit

creation system—provides the fuel for tail risk eruptions. When a spark ignites, such as the 2015 
deleveraging policy, the accumulated leverage and interconnected exposures combust into system-
wide contagion. In contrast, the entity-driven channel operates continuously through supply chain 
linkages, reflected in persistent price risk network structures, and proves less sensitive to these 
macro-financial vulnerabilities. This structural distinction explains the divergent activation patterns, 
transmission mechanisms, and crisis sensitivities documented across our analyses.

4.3.3. Robustness tests 
In our baseline regression, the multidimensional risk coupled networks are constructed at a 
weekly frequency using a 5-trading-day step length. Recognizing that networks built upon different 
step lengths are designed to capture inter-industry risk contagion over varying time horizons, we 
conduct a key robustness check to validate our baseline results and ensure they are not contingent 
on this specific methodological choice. Specifically, we reconstruct the multidimensional coupled 
networks using alternative step lengths of 10, 20, 30, 50, 100 and 120 trading days. All frequency-
dependent variables are adjusted accordingly. For instance, the RMB exchange rate volatility 
(FXVol) is recalculated as the realized volatility derived from daily RMB-USD exchange rate 
returns over forward-rolling windows of 10, 20, 30, 50, 100 and 120 trading days, respectively, and 
then scaled by 100. The industry price-to-earnings ratio (PEratio) and industry trading volume 
(Trade), based on the value-weighted price-to-earnings ratio of constituent firms and the natural 
logarithm of total trading volume, respectively, are recomputed as averages over these 
corresponding periods. The remaining control variables are invariant to the step length, as they are 
measured at daily or month-end frequencies, thus requiring no adjustment. 
Table 7 represents the regression results using different step lengths for network construction. 
First, we use shorter-term step lengths of 10 and 20 trading days. The results, reported in Table 7 
Panel A, show that the coefficients of main independent variables remain similar to our baseline 
regressions, confirming our main findings. Second, we employ medium-term step lengths of 30 and 
50 trading days. We repeat the baseline regressions and present the results in Table 7 Panel B. The 
estimations yield similar results, with the key coefficients retaining their sign and statistical 
significance. Third, we use much longer step lengths of 100 and 120 trading days to construct the 
networks, the results of which are reported in Table 7 Panel C. We find that even at these lower 
frequencies, the baseline relationships hold: the coefficients on the input and output intensity 
network effects are still positive and statistically significant, while the non-significance of the

robust to a wide range of network construction frequencies.

5. Conclusions 
This study examines inter-industry risk contagion in China through a multi-dimensional 
coupled network framework spanning 2007-2023. By decomposing aggregate risk into five 



=== 第39页 ===
 
37 
dimensions and analyzing their distinct transmission patterns, we uncover a dual-track contagion 
mechanism that operates through fundamentally different channels with heterogeneous temporal 
dynamics, network topologies, and macroeconomic sensitivities. 
Our central finding is that inter-industry risk transmission follows two coexisting yet distinct 
tracks. The entity-driven channel, proxied by price risk, functions as a structural backbone. It 
exhibits persistent high edge uniqueness and remarkably stable coupling weights (approximately 
0.20) throughout the sample period, reflecting continuous cost and demand propagation along 
supply chain networks. This structural channel maintains stable sectoral contributions, with cyclical 
industries consistently dominating transmission regardless of market conditions. 
In contrast, the financial-driven channel, proxied by tail risk, remains dormant during tranquil 
periods but activates explosively during systemic crises. Dynamic coupling weight analysis reveals 
a critical mechanism for distinguishing crisis types: during the 2015 stock market crash, tail risk 
weights surged to dominate aggregate connectivity, coinciding with peak edge uniqueness to signal 
complete channel activation driven by leverage unwinding. Conversely, during the COVID-19 
pandemic, a divergence emerged: while tail risk uniqueness rose, coupling weights remained 
moderate, indicating that the shock propagated primarily through supply chains rather than systemic 
financial dominance. This evidence confirms that the dominant transmission mechanism switches 
between entity-based and leverage-amplified pathways depending on whether the shock originates 
from the real economy or the financial system. 
Static case studies further validate this mechanism. The China-US trade friction period 
exemplifies entity-driven contagion: technology sectors cohesively absorbed risks through supply 
chain disruptions, transmission followed production linkages, and tail risk networks remained 
subdued. Conversely, the 2015 crash demonstrates financial-driven contagion: non-bank financial 
institutions became dominant risk propagators, technology sectors fragmented as leverage exposure 
superseded production relationships, and bidirectional amplification loops replaced unidirectional 
supply chain flows. Network autoregressive analysis identifies the macroeconomic roots of this 
asymmetry. Financialization degree and real estate prosperity significantly amplify tail risk 
contagion while exerting negligible influence on price risk transmission, indicating that the financial 
channel’s fragility stems from China’s structural dependence on leverage and property-backed 
credit creation. 
These findings carry implications for financial regulation and systemic risk management. Our 
evidence suggests that effective risk prevention requires a dual-track regulatory approach aligned 
with the distinct characteristics of each contagion channel. 
For the entity-driven channel, regulators should establish supply-chain-based early-warning 
systems leveraging industry price indices and production network centrality metrics. Given this 
channel’s persistent, structural nature, monitoring should focus on routine risk management rather 
than crisis-specific interventions. Price risk networks maintain high connectivity across all periods, 
with cyclical industries consistently serving as transmission hubs. Accordingly, surveillance 
systems should track cost propagation along upstream-downstream linkages, identify industries 
whose production centrality renders them systemically important for real-economy stability, and 
implement targeted support during supply disruptions to prevent cascading failures through 



=== 第40页 ===
 
38 
manufacturing networks. This constitutes continuous, non-crisis-specific oversight aimed at 
maintaining operational resilience in the real economy. 
For the financial-driven channel, regulators must prioritize macroprudential policies targeting 
financial leverage and real estate financing to prevent tail risk activation. Our NAR results 
demonstrate that financialization degree and property prosperity significantly amplify contagion 
intensity, providing fuel that ignites system-wide crises once triggered by deleveraging shocks or 
panic episodes. Regulatory focus should concentrate on financial institutions’ leverage ratios, 
shadow banking exposures, and real estate credit concentrations. Given this channel’s crisis-
activated nature, interventions should emphasize preemptive containment of leverage buildup and 
rapid stabilization when tail dependencies threaten to cascade. During normal periods, 
macroprudential tools should constrain excessive credit growth and speculative financing. During 
stress episodes, liquidity provision and orderly deleveraging protocols become critical to preventing 
the explosive amplification documented in our 2015 crash analysis. This constitutes crisis 
prevention at its core. 
Single-track regulatory frameworks that treat all risk dimensions equivalently will prove 
inadequate for managing systemic risk in emerging markets characterized by both persistent supply 
chain interdependencies and episodic financial instabilities. The dual-track approach acknowledges 
that different shock origins require differentiated policy responses: structural vulnerabilities demand 
continuous monitoring and gradual adjustment, whereas leverage-driven fragilities necessitate 
aggressive preemptive action to forestall nonlinear escalation. Our findings from China’s experience 
offer broader lessons for emerging economies undergoing financial deepening while maintaining 
extensive real-economy production networks, where the coexistence of entity-driven and financial-
driven contagion channels creates distinct regulatory challenges that conventional single-dimension
