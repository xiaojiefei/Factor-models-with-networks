A B S T R A C T
Causality is a widely-used concept in theoretical and empirical economics. The recent financial
economics literature has used the standard Granger causality to detect for the presence
of contemporaneous links among financial institutions, that, in turn, determine a network
structure. Subsequent studies have combined the estimated networks with traditional pricing
or risk measurement models to improve their fit to empirical data. In this paper, we provide
two contributions. First, we show how to use a linear factor model as a device for estimating
a combination of several networks that monitor the links across variables from different
viewpoints. Second, we highlight the advantages of combining quantile-based methods with
the Granger causality when the focus is on risk propagation. The empirical evidence supports
our contributions.
1. Introduction
A growing strand of the financial economics literature, related to financial contagion analyses, has investigated the role of
links among financial institutions as channels along which shocks spread through the financial system. These links might be read
as financial networks. The early literature on financial networks has analysed the transmission channel through which financial
contagion spreads within the financial system. The main objective was therefore to identify financial networks by using appropriate
criteria to detect links among the involved institutions. Nevertheless, this approach presupposed that such networks were, by
definition, unique. Acemoglu et al. (2012) used a network structure based on input–output relationships and showed that aggregate
fluctuations may originate from microeconomic shocks to firms. Kelly et al. (2013) showed how stock volatility relates to the
customer–supplier connectedness. Billio et al. (2014) used a contingent claim analysis and network measures to underscore the
links among sovereign, bank and insurance credit spreads. Moreover, there is a growing number of works proposing competing or
alternative approaches for estimating networks among financial institutions, markets, countries and (not necessarily financial) assets.
Among them, we refer to Billio et al. (2012), Diebold and Yilmaz (2014, 2015), Hautsch et al. (2012, 2013, 2014, 2015), Barigozzi
and Brownlees (2019), Ozdagli and Weber (2017) and Corsi et al. (2015). All these contributions have in common that they refer
to either a financial or an economic playground.
Despite the growing interest in the financial network topic, and the increasing understanding that there might be different
channels over which financial contagion spreads, the literature on combinations of financial or economic networks is still limited.

In fact, the different approaches used to estimate networks highlight different potential channels for the spread of both contagion and
risk. As a result, they might co-exist within the same financial system. It is then important to allow for the possibility of combining
them, obtaining a more complete picture of the risk spreading within the financial system. This paper provides a possible solution
building on a multiplex network, that is, a collection of networks (the so-called layers) existing among a set of institutions, that
we formally define in Section 2. Ideally, the constituents of a multiplex network represent the outcome of different approaches
used to either estimate or identify the links among the analysed companies. The literature on multiplex networks is relatively
recent in finance and, to the best of our knowledge, includes only a few papers focusing on contagion as well as on topological
properties. Bargigli et al. (2015) analysed the Italian interbank market using a multiplex network, distinguishing between five
layers: unsecured overnight, unsecured short-term (up to 12 months), unsecured long-term, secured short-term and secured long-
term transactions. Bargigli et al. (2015) also considered an aggregated network, computed by accumulating the available layers.
Following the authors, each layer has a different topological property and persistence over time. However, only the overnight market
was representative of the aggregate network among all the layers. Léon et al. (2014) studied the Colombian sovereign security market
and built a multiplex network with three different layers corresponding to the three Colombian environments trading and registering
sovereign securities. They found a strong nonlinear effect for the aggregate network value due to the fact that the principal layer
did not transfer its properties to the aggregate network or multiplex. Montagna and Kok (2013) developed a multi-layered interbank
network model by using an agent-based setting and focusing on a sample of large EU banks. The authors detected the existence of
non-negligible nonlinear effects influencing the shock propagation across individual banks. In other words, when different layers
were considered simultaneously, the contagion effect was larger than the sum of the contagion-induced losses observed when these
layers were analysed separately. Finally, Molina-Balboa et al. (2014) analysed the multiplex structure of bank exposures within the
Mexican banking system. They associate layers with different financial instruments and provide insights on the interdependence
among banks, with relevant implications from a systemic risk perspective.
By postulating the existence of a multiplex network, we take a step forward and combine the information contained in the layers,
analysing their impact and role within a financial economic model. In particular, we focus on the linear factor model augmented with
network dependence introduced by Billio et al. (2017). We show how the model can be generalized to accommodate for the presence
of a multiplex network. Furthermore, we provide a model parametrization which offers two useful interpretations of the estimation
output. First, the model parameters enable the statistical significance of the information contained in each layer to emerge. This
highlights the relevance of the various approaches in measuring and identifying a network within the model, providing an interesting
financial interpretation. This analysis might be relevant for policy purposes and market monitoring, as it might identify the most
relevant risk-spreading channels. Second, the estimated model parameters reveal a composite network obtained by combining the
multiplex network layers. As a result, the model-based combination provides an overall picture of the links existing among the
analysed variables, as measured by using different approaches (i.e., those behind the various layers of the network), as well as
accounting for their respective relevance.
The model-based combination of networks represents our first important contribution. Our second relevant contribution concerns
the estimation of networks. Several authors have already discussed possible ways to obtain a network of financial assets, as we said
above. Among the various methods, the Granger causality approach used by Billio et al. (2012) is taken as our starting point.
However, when focusing on the risk dimension, we typically focus on variances or, more generally, on systemic and/or systematic
risk measures. Nevertheless, Granger causality detects relations in mean. As a result, we do not believe that Granger causality is the
most appropriate method to quantify the risk interconnections among financial assets. This prompts us to discuss a set of alternative
approaches to estimate financial networks based on causality relationships that go beyond the classical Granger causality. We survey
a collection of methods that have a common denominator: they all resort to the quantile regression approach of Koenker and Bassett
(1978), in either a parametric or a nonparametric representation. By using these methods, we show how we can identify causality
among quantiles of the modelled variables. These quantile-based causality detection methods lead to the estimation of causality
networks mimicking the approach of Billio et al. (2012), but oriented towards a risk dimension. This is immediate when focusing
on the left quantiles of a random variable. As a further approach, we also show that the nonparametric quantile causality test of Jeong
et al. (2012) represents a further alternative to the traditional Granger causality for building causality networks. It is important to
highlight that, although the implemented methods focus on the same object, that is, a given 𝜏th quantile of the response variable,
they differ in capturing the causal impact of the explanatory variable and, therefore, provide a different source of information. In
particular, when using the standard quantile regression (Koenker and Bassett, 1978) we detect a linear impact of the explanatory
variable using its entire distribution. In contrast, we allow for a nonlinear impact by using the non-parametric causality test (Jeong
et al., 2012). Finally, when using the quantile-on-quantile method, we allow for a linear impact on the 𝜏th quantile of the response
variable (𝑦𝑡), but focusing on the neighbourhood of the 𝜃th quantile of the explanatory variable (𝑥𝑡).
We stress that, by focusing on the quantiles of the modelled variables, we have a clear risk interpretation about the estimated
networks. Indeed, the left quantiles are closely related to traditional risk measures, such as the Value-at-Risk and the Expected
Shortfall. Moreover, these networks will monitor the spreading of shocks or the links among the modelled variables when they
are in a high-risk state, and not when they are in a mean (or median) state, as captured by the Granger causality. Our purpose is
consistent with two other recent contributions. We refer to Hong et al. (2009) and Corsi et al. (2015), who focus on causality among
tail events. The Granger causality in risk in Hong et al. (2009), and also used by Corsi et al. (2015), is based on the occurrence
of tail events and detects the possible causality among such events. We might associate the tail with a conditional quantile and
estimate it with quantile regressions. We differ from the previous papers in that we focus on the causality within the assessment of
the conditional quantiles, not on the occurrence of tail events. We thus consider the causality on the risk measures rather than the
causality on the occurrence of extreme events.

We suggest different approaches to build causality networks, which involve monitoring the links across assets with views that
might complement the (mean-based) Granger causality. These competing networks represent the input for our model-based network
combination. The model also allows us to identify the relevance and the role of the various causality networks. We empirically
validate our two main proposals concerning the use of quantile causality-to infer the network structure across a set of (financial)
variables – and the model-based combination of causality networks. By using three different datasets – US industrial portfolios,
banks and insurance companies—and focusing on two different samples, that include, respectively, the periods 2006–2008 (affected
by the global financial crisis) and 2011–2015, we provide evidence of the different network structures derived from Granger and
quantile causality.
Our results suggest that quantile causality networks are denser than Granger causality networks. This is a relevant finding in
terms of systemic risk interpretation. Indeed, a denser network points out a much larger set of links and, thus, a possibly greater
systemic effect of shocks. The networks derived from the nonparametric quantile causality test of Jeong et al. (2012) also have a
clear core–periphery structure, which might help us to identify the most systemically relevant companies or sectors. Moreover, by
resorting to the model-based combination approach, we show how the combined networks have a different relevance across the
different datasets. In particular, the quantile causality methods turn out to be more relevant than the standard Granger causality
in the resulting composite network. In addition, the linear factor model augmented with a plurality of networks provides residuals
that are, on average, less correlated when compared to either the standard linear factor models or the network augmented linear
factor model proposed by Billio et al. (2017), which is based on the Granger causality only. Furthermore, the risk factor exposures
sensibly change when contemporaneous dependencies across assets are taken into account. Finally, we complement our findings
by showing that, when focusing on the risk dimension, a simple long-short portfolio strategy building on the combined network
centrality outperforms similar strategies based on a single network.
The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 discusses the approach we propose to obtain a model-based composite network,
extending the model of Billio et al. (2017). Section 3 provides the empirical findings, focusing on both the estimation of quantile
causality networks and the model-based combination of networks. Section 3 also includes a set of robustness checks. Section 4
concludes. A web appendix accompanies the paper; it includes additional tables and graphs, along with further methodological
notes.
2. Model-based network combination
In this section, we introduce how we obtain a model-based combination of a set of different networks. We assume that all
the networks are available and thus we do not estimate the structure of the various networks, but focus only on their optimal
combination. We start by formally defining both a simple and a multiplex or multilayer network.
A network (or graph) 𝐺= (𝑉, 𝐸) is a collection of vertices (or nodes) 𝑉and edges (or links) 𝐸, where 𝐸⊆(𝑉× 𝑣), typically
represented by using a 𝐾×𝐾adjacency matrix 𝑊, where 𝐾is the number of nodes. In particular, 𝑊is a binary matrix in which each
element 𝑤𝑖,𝑗can take only the values of 0 and 1. When 𝑤𝑖,𝑗= 1, node 𝑖is linked to node 𝑗, whereas 𝑤𝑖,𝑗= 0 points out the absence of
such relation. We set 𝑤𝑖,𝑖= 0, as we exclude self-loops, for 𝑖= 1, … , 𝐾. When 𝑊is symmetric, the corresponding network is defined
as undirected. In this case, the direction of links is not relevant, as they represent mutual relationships between a pair of nodes. In
contrast, when 𝑊is not symmetric, the corresponding network is defined as directed. In this case, 𝑤𝑖,𝑗= 1 points out an influence
of node 𝑖on node 𝑗, whereas we do not observe any impact from 𝑖to 𝑗when 𝑤𝑖,𝑗= 0. Note that 𝑤𝑖,𝑗is not necessarily equal to
𝑤𝑗,𝑖in directed networks, for 𝑖≠𝑗. When focusing on undirected networks, we can compute, for each node, two different measures,
defined, respectively, as outdegree – i.e., the number of outgoing links starting from a specific node – and indegree—i.e., the sum of
the ingoing links on the node of interest. We can also relax the assumption that 𝑤𝑖,𝑗can take only two possible values. Indeed, we
can allow 𝑤𝑖,𝑗to take values in the (positive) real axis. In this case, a network is defined as weighted (as opposed to unweighted)
because its links have also a size/relevance. As a result, each edge simply represents the presence of a connection in unweighted
networks, whereas, in weighted networks, edges also contain information about the strength of the relations among nodes.
It is worth noting the parallelism between the adjacency matrix and the spatial (proximity) matrix used in spatial econometrics.
The latter describes how the subjects of an analysis are spatially related. In such a different framework, the distance between subjects
is (generally) measured on a physical (natural) playground and connections correspond to neighbourhood relationships. Therefore,
if the element 𝑤𝑖,𝑗is equal to 1, the element 𝑗is a neighbour of the element 𝑖, following the spatial econometric nomenclature. The
spatial matrices are symmetric in standard applications, as neighbouring relationships are symmetric. It is also common practice
to focus on proximity matrices that are row-normalized. These matrices can then be interpreted as directed weighted networks, as
symmetrical relations might become asymmetrical after normalization.
Within a financial framework, networks are generally directed and weighted when they are measured by means of balance sheet
quantities. For instance, Billio et al. (2016) used BIS cross–holdings, Diebold and Yilmaz (2014) developed an approach based on
variance decompositions of target series, Acemoglu et al. (2012) used the Input–Output matrix. A network might also be directed
and unweighted. Relevant examples are the networks obtained using Granger causality (Billio et al., 2012) or Granger Causality in
the tails (Corsi et al., 2015). A network might even be unweighted and undirected, as in the economic sector case of Caporin and
Paruolo (2015).

This work aims to combine financial networks by building on a variant of a financial economic model, that is, the linear
factor model augmented with a spatial link, following the approach of Billio et al. (2017). The advantage of our model lies in
that the combination of the various layers of the multiplex network is weighted with a set of coefficients appearing in the model
parametrization. As well as representing the elements for a convex linear combination of layers, the coefficients would then enable
the significance and relevance of each layer to be assessed. We briefly review the model proposed by Billio et al. (2017) in the next
subsection. Here, we also introduce the model generalization in the presence of a multilayer network. We then discuss the approach
used to derive a composite network from the model estimates.

the heterogeneous reaction of the assets to the composite network. This makes the multiple-network model comparable, in terms of

composite.

2.2. Causality testing and causality networks
The concept of causality and the tools used to test for its existence date back to the seminal contributions of Granger (1969,
1980, 1988). The purpose of his original contribution was to identify causal relationships in mean across economic variables, and
his work attracted considerable interest in the econometrics literature; see, e.g., Geweke (1984) and Hoover (2001). This method
was later extended to the analysis of causality in variance. Granger et al. (1986) provided a first definition of causality in variance,
whereas Comte and Lieberman (2000) extended and generalized this concept. On the testing side, Cheung and Ng (1996) proposed a
test based on cross-correlations, Hafner (2003) introduced a procedure based on the likelihood of competing models, whereas Hafner
and Herwartz (2008) presented a Wald-type approach. More recently, contributions on causality between two random variables,
that go beyond the first– and second-order moments, have been proposed. In particular, we refer to causality in quantiles – see,
e.g., the nonparametric test of Jeong et al. (2012) and the work of Lee and Yang (2014) – and in distribution—see, e.g., Candelon
and Topkavi (2016).
The use of Granger causality in estimating financial networks stems from the work done by Billio et al. (2012). The authors
associated the statistical evidence of Granger causality in mean with the existence of an edge connecting two assets (or nodes). In
a context of market turmoil or when the focus is on systemic risk spreading after local or specific shocks, the standard causality in
mean might not be enough, and should be combined with a causality testing that covers the risk dimension. Appendix A provides a
brief review of the methods we use in the empirical analysis. First, we describe the approach of Billio et al. (2012), based on Granger
causality. We then move to causality in quantiles, focusing on three different approaches: (i) a Granger-type causality building on
the baseline quantile regression introduced by Koenker and Bassett (1978); (ii) a generalization of the baseline quantile regression,
accounting also for the location of the conditioning (or explanatory) variable over its support, defined by Sim and Zhou (2015) as
quantile–on-quantile; and (iii) the nonparametric quantile causality test introduced by Jeong et al. (2012).
As discussed in the introduction, the lower quantiles of stock returns acquire a more relevant role within a financial framework, as
they are interpreted as Values-at-Risk. The availability of a number of competing methods for estimating causality networks enables
the construction of a multiplex network, in which each layer is associated with a specific network. The multiplex network based on
causality links can then be used for the model-based construction of composite networks, as discussed in the previous section. The
same criteria apply to the estimation of a single causality network, irrespective of the approach used to identify causality links. It is
also important to note that we estimate a causality network starting from historical information on 𝐾assets, generally focusing on
their returns. As a result, the 𝐾assets represent the network nodes. Our purpose is to identify edges connecting each pair of nodes.
If all assets were connected, when excluding self-loops, we would have a total number of 𝐾2 −𝐾edges in which case the associated
adjacency matrix is symmetric if the network is unweighted.

3.2. Granger and quantile causality networks
In building the causality networks, we extend the existing literature by combining the standard Granger testing approach with
methods based on dependencies across quantiles, as described in Appendix A.1. The methods based on quantile causality depend on
the quantile at which the analysis is performed. Therefore, we must first specify which quantiles we use to conduct the dependence or
causality analysis. Our analysis illustrates the changes occurring in the causality networks when switching from Granger to quantile
causality, without identifying the optimal quantile level over which to measure quantile causality. We therefore decided to fix the
quantiles of interest a priori. In particular, we chose three quantile levels: 0.1, 0.5 and 0.9, corresponding to the tails and to the
centre of the returns distributions. Note that the approach of Sim and Zhou (2015) depends on two quantiles: the 𝜏th (for the
response variable) and the 𝜃th (for the explanatory variable) quantiles. We set both quantiles at the same level, that is, 𝜃= 𝜏(see
Appendix A for details on the quantile causality methods we consider).
When evaluating the quantile regression parameters, we estimate their standard errors by using the 𝑥𝑦-pair bootstrap method
(Efron and Tibshirani, 1993) with 5000 replications. This approach provides accurate results without assuming any particular
distribution for the error term. When a kernel function is involved (see Appendix A for details), we use the Gaussian one.
Following Balcilar et al. (2017) and Bonaccolto et al. (2018), we estimate the optimal bandwidths involved in the (Jeong et al.,
2012) test using the least squares cross-validation method of Rudemo (1982) and Bowman (1984). We also need to specify the lag
orders for both the dependent and the explanatory variables. To limit the computational burden, we fix a priori both lag orders
to be 1. Finally, as for the quantile-on-quantile method, the objective functions depend also on a bandwidth (see Appendix A for
details). Larger values of the bandwidth make smaller the variance of the estimates, but, on the other hand, would increase their
bias. The opposite holds when ℎtakes lower values. Optimizing the value of ℎby using ad-hoc statistical methods is still an open
issue in a quantile-on-quantile framework; we do not analyse this aspect in the present study and leave it for future research. In
contrast, we follow Sim and Zhou (2015) and use a plug-in bandwidth equal to 0.1. We remind that the bandwidth applies to a
quantile level. Our choice allows avoiding estimating the parameters with a small number of data points (as it is the case with
smaller bandwidths) and, at the same time, to keep the focus around the quantiles of interest. We set the confidence level at 5%
for judging the significance of the coefficients and testing the null hypothesis behind the tests for Granger or quantile causality. To
control for the possible impact of the common dependence on the market, we test for Granger causality as well as for the various
forms of quantile causality, also including the supercomposite US market index within the corresponding models.2
In the remainder of this paper, we identify the various networks that we estimate by means of acronyms. For networks estimated
using quantile regression approaches (the baseline case, the quantile-on-quantile and the nonparametric test), each acronym consists
of four or five characters. The first and the second ones identify the method: QB for baseline quantile regression, Qo for quantile-on-
quantile and QN for nonparametric quantile. The third and fourth characters identify the quantile. For instance, QB10 refers to the
network estimated by using the baseline quantile regression at the 10% quantile level. As for the Qo method, the reported quantile
is used for both the dependent and the explanatory variables. We use GR for the standard Granger causality networks. Finally, we
add a final letter F to the acronym when we use a common factor in the estimation of the causality networks.

Our method points at combining different networks, each of which should reflect a different view on causality. Therefore, before
moving to the network combination, we analyse the similarity of the individual networks. Indeed, combining very similar networks
might be of little interest, while we are most interested in combining networks carrying different information. We measure the
similarity of different networks with the Jaccard index (Jaccard, 1901), which takes values between 0 (maximum discordance) and
100 (perfect matching). We report the results in Table 2.6 In general, the Jaccard index takes low values for all the network pairs,
thus highlighting the usefulness of network combination. We highlight that the networks based on nonparametric quantile regression
and those derived from quantile-on-quantile tend to be more similar; see, e.g., the case of the 48 Industry Portfolios, for 𝜏= 0.1 and
for the period 2011–2015, where the Jaccard index is equal to 32.22. In contrast, the baseline quantile regression networks tend to
be closer to the Granger causality networks; see, e.g., the case of the 48 Industry Portfolios, for 𝜏= 0.5 and the period 2006–2008,
where the Jaccard index is equal to 34.46. Given these evidences, supporting the different views provided by alternative causality
networks, we proceed with the estimation of the composite network.
3.3. The composite causality network
Starting from the availability of causality networks derived from four different methods – QB, Qo, QN and GR (see Section 3.2)
– and over three quantile levels (10%, 50% and 90%), we now estimate the composite networks. We estimate the model in (4),
accounting for the presence of common factors, for which we follow the usual practice and use the market factor, the size factor,
the book–to-market factor and the momentum factor, following Fama and French (1993), Fama and French (1995) and Carhart
(1997). A priori, we have no information to suggest a possible preference for a particular network. We then estimate the composite
network starting from four different layers, where the quantile-based networks are associated with the same reference quantile. We
provide a sensitivity analysis on the effects of excluding a single network (among the four available layers) in Section 3.4, where we
report a set of robustness checks. When estimating factor models, it is common to use a monthly frequency. However, this would
leave us with only 36 (60) observations in the first (second) sample. We then estimate the linear factor model (augmented with
network dependencies) by using weekly returns. This choice allows us to increase the number of observations—108 in the first
sample and 316 in the second one. Moreover, this choice has advantages in terms of model estimation and parameter inference. We
do not consider daily returns as they would require the introduction of heteroskedastic dynamics in the variance of the residuals,
increasing the complexity of the estimation due to the well-known curse of dimensionality. Estimates of the network combination

The estimates of the linear factor model with network dependencies generate different outputs. First, we analyse the model
parameters. We refer to both the weight of each network, as measured by the 𝛿𝑖parameters, and the impact of the composite
network on the assets returns, i.e., the parameters included in . Second, we focus on the composite network, obtained by combining
the primitive networks weighted by the 𝛿𝑖coefficients. Third, we study the model residuals, which contain useful information for
assessing the advantages of moving to a composite network, as opposed to the benchmark cases—absence of network dependencies
(where the contemporaneous link matrix 𝐴is an identity matrix) or network dependencies captured by Granger causality. The
latter is a valuable benchmark as we would like to highlight the potential improvements of measuring the links among assets in
their quantiles, going beyond the mean. We compare the estimated models taking into account the average correlation of their
residuals. We obtain improvements when the residuals have a smaller correlation level, that is, more whitened residuals. Table 3
reports the parameters estimated for the combined network using the three datasets and over the periods 2006–2008 and 2011–
2015. Bearing in mind that the sum of these parameters is equal to 1 and that they take positive values, a larger coefficient points
out a greater relevance of the associated network. We also assess the significance of the estimated parameters. Focusing on the
banks dataset, we note that the nonparametric quantile causality network is the most relevant during the financial crisis period and
across all quantiles. Notably, the estimated 𝛿parameter for this network ranges between 0.736 and 0.883. Furthermore, it is always
statistically significant when the estimation of the networks is made with the presence of a common market factor.
We stress that, from a systemic risk perspective, this result suggests that the risk is more widespread across banks, as the
nonparametric quantile network is much denser than the other causality networks. This greater relevance of the nonparametric
quantile causality network also emerges for the median and for the lower and upper tails. In all cases, the Granger causality
network has a very small weight (which is non-significant in five cases out of six). When focusing on the period 2011–2015, both
the nonparametric quantile and the quantile-on-quantile causality networks are relevant, with and without the presence of common
market factors. Here again, although it is more statistically significant, the Granger causality network has smaller weights than the
quantile-based causality networks. Overall, in both samples, the baseline quantile regression network has the least relevant role.
Finally, in the second sample, we see a clear change in the combined network parameters when moving from median networks to
networks estimated on either the lower or upper tails. In particular, the impact of the quantile-on-quantile networks increases, thus
supporting the importance of looking at quantile causality too when accounting for interdependence across assets in a linear factor
model and showing that a double conditioning (on both the response and the explanatory variables) is important when estimating
causality at quantile levels.

Table 4 reports the summary measures for the combined networks. The various composite networks are similar in all the
quantities we report (for a given sample period and a given dataset). The identified heterogeneity is much smaller than the one
observed for the primitive networks. Figs. 3–5 display the combined networks for the period 2006–2008.7 We link this finding to the
use of a linear factor model augmented with network dependencies. The contemporaneous relations among the modelled variables
capture their correlations and go beyond what we associate with common factors. It might be that the various composite networks
capture the dependencies among the returns (beyond common factors) in a similar way. The differences we find depend partly on
the heterogeneity across networks and partly on the different weights assigned to the primitive networks.
To shed further light on the differences between the composite networks as well as on the improvements gained by the linear
factor model augmented with network combination, we provide in Tables 5–7 descriptive analyses of the correlations among the
residuals computed from (4). Tables 5–7 include two benchmarks: (i) a standard linear factor model, that is, the (Carhart, 1997)
4-factors CAPM; and (ii) a linear factor model augmented with network dependencies, where the network is derived from the
Granger causality (with or without the inclusion of a common factor in estimating causal relationships). The results obtained by
using the banks and the insurance companies datasets are similar. The residuals of the 4-factors CAPM have the largest median
correlations, with a distribution of these correlations shifted to the right and a clear prevalence of positive values. The introduction
of network dependencies, when measured by Granger causality, improves the model fit, but the predominance of positive values
in the correlation remains (see the small fraction of correlations below −0.1), and the median correlation remains above 0.1. The
model fit improves considerably with the introduction of a plurality of networks. Indeed, the residual correlations are centred at
zero, with a larger fraction of correlations below −0.1 and a marked reduction of large positive residual correlations. As a result, we
conclude that our approach based on combining several networks within a network augmented linear factor model overperforms
the standard Granger causality. The improvements achieved by the latter, over the multifactor model, were already documented
in Billio et al. (2017).

When analysing the BIC, we note that our combinations outperform GR. This highlights the advantages of using the causality
in quantiles rather than the Granger causality when building financial networks. The most general combination, Q(𝜏), is always
placed among the top-ranked approaches, highlighting the synergies coming from QB, Qo, QN and GR when combined in a unique
structure. When using the leave-one-out technique to compare the individual performance of QB, Qo and QN, respectively, we check
that QN provides the greatest contribution for banks and insurances during the period 2006–2008, characterized by a higher risk
during the subprime crisis. In contrast, Qo provides a better performance during the period 2011–2015 for 𝜏= {0.1, 0.9} (see Table 8
and Appendix F (Table F.4)), whereas QN becomes relevant for 𝜏= 0.5 (see Appendix F (Table F.3)).

We also evaluate the contribution of our network combination approach from a financial viewpoint, that is, in terms of portfolio
allocation. In particular, we analyse the performance of two different strategies. The first, SA, is made up with long positions on
stocks with lower centrality and short positions on stocks with higher centrality. The second, SB, is the opposite of SA: it is long
on stocks with high centrality and short on stocks with low centrality. We use these strategies to investigate when the centrality
improves the portfolio performance, according to crisis, boom and calm periods. Higher centrality stocks could be difficult to
diversify, because they are connected to other stocks, inflating the idiosyncratic portfolio variance. In contrast, lower centrality
stocks could be useful, as they are weakly correlated to other stocks through the links in the estimated network. For each of our
datasets, we sort the stocks according to their centrality and cluster them in quintiles. We then implement SA and SB by using only
the stocks included in the first and in the fifth quintiles. In particular, SA is made up with equally weighted long (short) positions
on the stocks included in the first (fifth) quintile. The opposite holds for SB. For both SA and SB, the number of stocks in either the
first or the fifth quintile depends on the dataset we are using: five for banks and insurances and ten for the 48 industry portfolios.
For both SA and SB, the stocks centrality is retrieved from the networks we defined above: Q(𝜏), Q(𝜏)\QN, Q(𝜏)\Qo, Q(𝜏)\QB and
GR. We then compare the contribution of each network to the performance of the resulting portfolio. Furthermore, we compare
SA and SB with the equally weighted portfolio (EW); see, e.g., DeMiguel et al. (2009), Duchin and Levy (2009) and Tu and Zhou
(2011). We compare the competing strategies by using three performance indicators: the standard deviation of the portfolio returns
(STD), the Sharpe ratio (SR) and the Jensen’s 𝛼retrieved from the Fama and French (1993)’s model estimated for each portfolio
strategy (̂𝛼). Moreover, we also check whether the variances and the Sharpe ratios of both SA and SB are statistically different from
the ones generated by EW by using the tests proposed by Ledoit and Wolf (2008, 2011). We report in Table 9 the results obtained
at 𝜏= 0.1. For the sake of space, we report the results obtained at 𝜏= {0.5, 0.9} in Tables J.1–J.2 (see Appendix J). However, we
discuss below the findings retrieved from a joint analysis of all three quantile levels—𝜏= {0.1, 0.5, 0.9}.
Table 9 (as well as the additional results in Appendix J) points out that the most general combination, Q(𝜏), is always placed
among the top-ranked strategies for all the performance indexes. Again, this highlights the synergies coming from QB, Qo, QN and
GR, when combined in a unique network. SA and SB provide the same performance in terms of STD, as one of them is the opposite
of the other and the differences disappear when computing squared returns. When evaluating the individual contribution of the
three causality networks (QB, Qo and QN) by using the leave-one-out procedure, we check that none of the three methodologies
systematically dominates the others. In contrast, the results change according to the performance index (STD, SR or ̂𝛼), the dataset
(banks, insurances or industry portfolios), the strategy (SA or SB) and the period (2006–2008 or 2011–2015).
Finally, we compare the strategies building on the causality in quantiles networks with the one building on the Granger causality
network (GR). Many of the strategies retrieved from the causality in quantiles provide a lower standard deviation in the period
2006–2008, where the risk minimization acquires a critical importance due to the market turmoil generated by the subprime
crisis. This might be linked to the greater flexibility of these methods compared to the Granger causality. In contrast, GR provides
a lower standard deviation during the period 2011–2015, that is, during a recovery phase in which it is possible to implement
more aggressive strategies, also thanks to the sparsity of the Granger network. We read the latter as a consequence of the reduced
perception of systemic risks during the recovery phase.9 When using the banks dataset for 𝜏= {0.1, 0.9}, GR tends to have a better
performance in terms of Sharpe ratio in period 2006–2008. In contrast, many of the networks building on the causality in quantiles
have a better performance when using the insurances dataset in the same period. Moving to the period 2011–2015, many of the
networks obtained from the causality in quantiles provide a better performance in terms of Sharpe ratio. Similar results hold when
analysing ̂𝛼. When we compare the strategies building on the causality in quantiles with EW, Table 9 as well as the additional
results in Appendix J show that the strategies building on our combination (for both SA and SB) outperform EW in most of the
cases, in terms of STD, SR and ̂𝛼. The standard deviations of SA and SB are statistically different from the ones provided by EW in
the majority of cases.
To summarize, the analyses described above highlight the improvements provided by our combining methods when compared
to well-known benchmarks in the literature, under both a statistical and a financial viewpoint. The results highlight the capability
of our combinations in capturing the synergies of different methods designed to quantify different sources of causality in quantiles.
Among them, we highlight the contribution of QN, that builds on the nonparametric test proposed by Jeong et al. (2012).

Appendix K reports tables assessing the model-based network combination when we exclude a network. We consider two specific
cases. The first excludes the network estimated from Granger causality, in order to highlight the relevance of competing quantile
causality networks. The second disregards the less relevant quantile causality network (the so-called baseline quantile causality). All
the analyses are based on weekly data. The results confirm the relevance of the nonparametric approach for the purpose of obtaining
a quantile causality that, in several cases, generates the largest and statistically most significant coefficients. The baseline quantile
causality is the least relevant, even when the Granger causality network is excluded from the layers. The results for the industry
portfolios are the most heterogeneous in terms of network relevance and they show a little improvement in terms of contraction
of the residual correlations. For the banks and insurance companies, there is a marked gain in moving from linear factor models,
or Granger causality augmented factor models, to a model that accounts for the presence of quantile causality; indeed, the average
residual correlation decreases considerably.
Appendix G reports estimates of the network combination modelled on monthly data (as opposed to the weekly data considered in
the previous section).10 We confirm the importance of nonparametric quantile causality and the limited impact of Granger causality.
Once again, the outcome is clearer for the banks and insurances datasets. In addition, in contrast to the evidence emerging from
the weekly data, the contraction of the residual correlations for the insurance company dataset is less evident during the financial
crisis, whereas it is striking in the second period. Given the heterogeneity of the network impact on returns, as mentioned at the
end of the previous section, we study the model performance when we impose a common reaction of the returns to exposure to
the network. Appendix L illustrates the outcomes for the composite network and the impact of the network on returns in the three
datasets and two sample periods, when the model is estimated on weekly data. The only relevant changes concern the composite
network parameters: we now find a larger number of statistically significant coefficients. In addition, Granger causality seems to
become more relevant, in the insurance companies dataset at least. The nonparametric quantile causality network nonetheless retains
its role and receives the largest coefficients. Moving to the model-based combination confirms the previous results and enables a
reduction in the average residual correlations in the banks and insurance companies datasets.
4. Concluding remarks
Causality networks have attracted a relevant attention in the financial economics literature during the recent years for the
purposes of systemic risk interpretation. We show that the structure of causality networks changes considerably if we move from
the mean – commonly associated to the standard Granger causality – to the extreme quantiles. In the latter case, the causality
across assets can be measured by means of quantile regression-based approaches. Here we illustrate three different possibilities: the
traditional quantile regression introduced by Koenker and Bassett (1978), its generalization to the quantile–on-quantile approach
of Sim and Zhou (2015) and the nonparametric test due to Jeong et al. (2012). From a systemic risk perspective, we show that
moving to quantile-based approaches leads to denser networks with a broader systemic relevance. By focusing on a linear factor
model augmented with a multi-layer network dependence, we also demonstrate that causality networks are useful for capturing this
dependence across financial returns, going beyond what can be explained by a market factor. This paves the way to further analyses
and has an associated impact on diversification analyses and portfolio construction, as well as on asset pricing.
